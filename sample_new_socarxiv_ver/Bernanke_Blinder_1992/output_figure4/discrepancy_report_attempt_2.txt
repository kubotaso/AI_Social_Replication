Discrepancy Report -- Figure 4, Attempt 2
==========================================

Score: 30/100

Same fundamental problem as attempt 1: IRF magnitudes are much too large.

KEY DIAGNOSTICS:
- Funds rate innovation std dev: 0.3382 (paper says 0.31 -- close)
- Raw IRF deposits at month 12: -0.021420 (paper expects -0.008)
- Raw IRF unemployment at month 24: 0.541784 (paper expects 0.0017)
- All values are 2.5-3x too large for bank variables, and ~300x too large for unemployment

ANALYSIS:
The needed scale factor for unemployment is 0.31, which is remarkably close to the
funds_rate std dev (0.338). This suggests that the paper may be plotting the response
to a unit shock (1 pp) in the orthogonal funds rate innovation, not a 1-std-dev shock.
But if the shock were 0.31 pp, the responses should be smaller, not larger -- and the
discrepancy for bank variables is only 2.5x, not ~300x like unemployment.

POTENTIAL EXPLANATION:
1. The bank variable magnitudes being ~2.5x too large could be a data vintage issue or
   a variable definition mismatch (using checkable deposits instead of total deposits,
   using bank_investments instead of proper securities measure).

2. The unemployment magnitude being ~300x too large is pathological. This suggests
   that the paper may NOT include unemployment in the same VAR, or may be plotting
   unemployment on a completely different scale. But the figure clearly shows one axis.

3. Perhaps the key issue is that the paper uses SCALED impulse responses where the
   bank variables are multiplied by some constant (like their standard deviation)
   to put all responses on a comparable scale.

PLAN FOR ATTEMPT 3:
1. Try using pre-computed log_bank_loans_real, log_bank_investments_real, and
   log_bank_deposits_check_real from the dataset.
2. Try different variables to see if magnitudes change.
3. Consider that perhaps the x10^-2 axis label means the plotted values ARE the
   raw values and there's no additional scaling needed. In that case, don't multiply
   by 100, and the deposits raw value of -0.021 would be plotted directly.
   The axis would show something like -0.02, which is close to the paper's -0.8
   when multiplied by... no, that still doesn't work.
4. Try reducing the log bank variables by their standard deviation (normalization).
5. Consider that the paper uses log(variable / (CPI/100)) where CPI is rescaled.
