Table 1 — "Marginal Significance Levels of Monetary Indicators for Forecasting Alternative Measures of Economic Activity: Six-Variable Prediction Equations"

Source: Bernanke, Ben S. and Alan S. Blinder (1992). "The Federal Funds Rate and the Channels of Monetary Transmission." The American Economic Review, 82(4), 901-921.

================================================================================
STRUCTURE
================================================================================

The table shows p-values (marginal significance levels) for F-tests of excluding
6 lags of each monetary/interest rate variable from an OLS prediction equation.

Each equation includes:
  - A constant
  - 6 lags of the dependent variable
  - 6 lags of log CPI
  - 6 lags of log M1
  - 6 lags of log M2
  - 6 lags of BILL (3-month T-bill rate)
  - 6 lags of BOND (10-year Treasury bond rate)
  - 6 lags of FUNDS (federal funds rate)

Total regressors per equation: 1 + 6*7 = 43

Each column in the table tests the joint significance of dropping that column's
variable (its 6 lags) from the equation. The reported values are p-values from
the F-test.

All real variables and CPI are in log levels.
Interest rates (BILL, BOND, FUNDS) are in levels (annualized percent).

Panel A: Sample 1959:7-1989:12
Panel B: Sample 1959:7-1979:9 (pre-Volcker)

Columns: M1, M2, BILL, BOND, FUNDS

Row variables (9 forecasted/dependent variables):
  1. Industrial production
  2. Capacity utilization
  3. Employment
  4. Unemployment rate
  5. Housing starts
  6. Personal income
  7. Retail sales
  8. Consumption
  9. Durable-goods orders

================================================================================
NOTES FROM THE PAPER
================================================================================

- Data are monthly.
- M1, M2, industrial production, employment, and housing starts are in log levels.
- Personal income, retail sales, and consumption are deflated by CPI and in log levels.
- Capacity utilization is in levels (percent), NOT in logs.
  (The paper lists it among forecasted variables but it is a rate, like unemployment.)
- Unemployment rate is in levels (percent), NOT in logs.
- Data from DRI database (Data Resources Inc., now IHS Markit/S&P Global).
- FUNDS = Federal funds rate (effective rate, monthly average).
- BILL = 3-month Treasury bill rate (secondary market rate, monthly average).
- BOND = 10-year government bond rate (constant maturity, monthly average).
- Sample starts 1959:7 because money supply data begins 1959:1 and 6 lags are needed.
- Panel B ends 1979:9, just before Paul Volcker became Fed Chair (October 1979).

================================================================================
SIGNIFICANCE PATTERNS (for validation)
================================================================================

Key findings from the paper:
- FUNDS is consistently the most significant predictor of real activity.
- M1 has essentially no marginal predictive power once interest rates are included.
- M2 is occasionally significant but less consistently than FUNDS.
- BILL is significant for employment and unemployment in both samples.
- BOND is generally less significant than FUNDS.
- The federal funds rate dominates other monetary indicators as a predictor.
