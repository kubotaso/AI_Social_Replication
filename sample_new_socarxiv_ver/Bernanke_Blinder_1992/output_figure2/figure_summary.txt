Figure 2 — "Responses of Funds Rate to Inflation and Unemployment Shocks"

Source: Bernanke and Blinder (1992), Figure 2, p. 911

=== DESCRIPTION ===

Figure 2 shows two impulse response functions plotted in a single panel (stacked vertically):
1. Top curve: "Response to Inflation" — the response of the federal funds rate to a one-standard-deviation inflation shock
2. Bottom curve: "Response to Unemployment" — the response of the federal funds rate to a one-standard-deviation unemployment shock

=== MODEL SPECIFICATION ===

- Model: 3-variable VAR with 6 lags
- Variables: FUNDS (federal funds rate), unemployment rate (male 25-54), log CPI
- Choleski ordering: [FUNDS, unemployment rate, log CPI]
  (Footnote 28: "The ordering is: policy variable, unemployment rate, inflation rate.")
- Sample period: pre-October 1979 (approximately 1959:7 to 1979:9)
- Shocks: one-standard-deviation orthogonalized shocks (Choleski decomposition)

=== VISUAL CHARACTERISTICS ===

- X-axis: "HORIZON (MONTHS)" from about 2 to 24
- Y-axis: ranges from approximately -0.3 to 0.5
- Both curves plotted on the same axes

Response to Inflation curve:
- Starts at 0 at horizon 0
- Rises steeply to a peak of approximately 0.4-0.45 at about 5-10 months
- Then slowly decays but remains positive through month 24
- Value at month 24 is approximately 0.2-0.3

Response to Unemployment curve:
- Starts at 0 at horizon 0
- Drops slightly and then becomes progressively more negative
- Reaches approximately -0.1 to -0.15 by month 24
- Much flatter and smaller in magnitude than the inflation response

=== APPROXIMATE NUMERICAL VALUES (read from figure) ===

Response to Inflation (funds rate response to one-SD inflation shock):
  Month 0:  ~0.00
  Month 3:  ~0.20
  Month 6:  ~0.35-0.40
  Month 9:  ~0.40-0.45 (near peak)
  Month 12: ~0.35-0.40
  Month 18: ~0.30
  Month 24: ~0.25

Response to Unemployment (funds rate response to one-SD unemployment shock):
  Month 0:  ~0.00
  Month 3:  ~-0.02
  Month 6:  ~-0.05
  Month 9:  ~-0.07
  Month 12: ~-0.10
  Month 18: ~-0.12
  Month 24: ~-0.13

=== KEY FEATURES ===

1. Inflation shocks drive up the funds rate, with peak effect at approximately 5-10 months
2. Unemployment shocks push the funds rate in the opposite direction (negative), with somewhat longer lags and smaller magnitudes
3. Footnote 29: "A one-standard-deviation inflation shock is a much bigger number (215 basis points, at an annual rate) than a one-standard-deviation unemployment shock (18 basis points)."
4. The inflation response is much larger in magnitude than the unemployment response, consistent with the Fed reacting more aggressively to inflation
5. Both responses are persistent — effects remain visible through the full 24-month horizon

- "Response to Unemployment" label near the bottom curve
- Clean line plot style typical of AER publications circa 1992
- No confidence bands shown in the original figure
