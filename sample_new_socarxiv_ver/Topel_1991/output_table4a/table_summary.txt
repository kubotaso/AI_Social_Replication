TABLE 4A: Relationship between Remaining Job Duration and Current Wage Growth

Source: Topel (1991), "Specific Capital, Mobility, and Wages: Wages Rise with Job Seniority,"
Journal of Political Economy, Vol. 99, No. 1, pp. 145-176.

==============================================================================
DATA AND SAMPLE
==============================================================================

Data source: Panel Study of Income Dynamics (PSID), 1968-1983
Sample: White males
This table tests whether current wage growth is related to how long the worker
will remain on the job in the future (remaining job duration).

==============================================================================
TABLE STRUCTURE
==============================================================================

Type: Regression coefficients from augmented first-step model
Layout: 2 rows

Row 1: Single coefficient on remaining job duration (linear)
Row 2: Separate effects for jobs ending in each future period (t+1 through t+6)

The baseline first-step model (Table 2) is augmented with remaining job
duration as an additional regressor. If wage growth reflects true returns
to seniority rather than selection bias, current wage growth should not
predict future job separation.

==============================================================================
GROUND-TRUTH VALUES (standard errors in parentheses)
==============================================================================

Row 1: Remaining Job Duration (linear)
  Coefficient: .0006 (.0010)

Row 2: Separate effects for jobs ending in each period
  (Omitted category: jobs that survive 6 or more years)

  t+1:  -.012 (.012)
  t+2:  -.015 (.013)
  t+3:   .013 (.013)
  t+4:   .012 (.014)
  t+5:   .020 (.015)
  t+6:   .004 (.017)

==============================================================================
NOTES
==============================================================================

- Omitted category is jobs that survive 6 or more additional years
- An interaction of remaining duration with a dummy for jobs that censor
  at the end of the panel was also included but had no effect
- None of the coefficients is statistically significant, supporting the
  interpretation that within-job wage growth reflects genuine returns to
  seniority rather than selection effects
- The remaining job duration variable measures how many more years the
  worker stays on the current job after period t
- This is a specification test: if wage growth were driven by match quality
  (selection) rather than true seniority returns, we would expect a positive
  relationship between remaining duration and current wage growth

==============================================================================
STATISTICAL METHOD
==============================================================================

The first-step regression from Table 2 is augmented with remaining job
duration as an additional explanatory variable. The dependent variable
is still the change in log real wage. The test examines whether future
job duration predicts current wage growth, conditional on the standard
first-step regressors (tenure and experience polynomial terms plus time
dummies).
