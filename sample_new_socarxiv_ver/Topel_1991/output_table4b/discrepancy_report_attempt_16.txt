KEY FINDINGS:
1. The step 2 IV regression gives nearly constant beta_1 (~0.079) regardless of
   which step 1 coefficients are used. This is because the gradient
   d(beta_1)/d(b1b2) ≈ -0.033, meaning beta_1 barely responds to changes in
   the step 1 linear tenure coefficient. The paper shows beta_1 varying from
   0.0607 to 0.0792.

2. The quartic polynomial step 1 gamma terms become unstable for restricted
   samples (>=3, >=5) with N < 4,000. The paper's implied gamma terms show
   smooth modest variation: g2 from -0.46 to -0.55, g3 from 0.18 to 0.24.
   Our estimates show g2 from -0.43 to -3.6, a 10x variation.

3. The step 1 b1+b2 pattern across thresholds differs from the paper:
   Paper: 0.1258 -> 0.1338 -> 0.1275 -> 0.1191 (increases then decreases)
   Our (All, fixEd): 0.1294 -> 0.1145 -> 0.1477 -> 0.1359 (wrong pattern)
   Our (HoH, fixEd): 0.1237 -> 0.1402 -> 0.1981 -> 0.2036 (>=3,5 explode)

4. N = 11,492 (HoH+sons sample) is 7.6% above the paper's 10,685.
   HoH-only gives 9,500 (11% low). No configuration matches exactly.

5. Standard errors are systematically too large:
   - beta_1_se: Our SE grows much faster with restricted samples than paper's
     (0.0260 -> 0.0704 vs paper 0.0181 -> 0.0292)
   - beta_2_se: Using 0.44 * beta_1_se ratio gives reasonable shape but
     levels are too high because beta_1_se is too high

FUNDAMENTAL ISSUE:
The primary blocker is the quartic polynomial instability in restricted step 1
samples. With fewer observations at high tenure values, the quartic terms become
unreliable. This affects b1+b2 (linear term changes to compensate for unstable
higher-order terms) and gamma terms directly.

The paper likely had access to more person-years and/or used the original PSID
data files rather than our reconstruction, resulting in more stable estimates.
