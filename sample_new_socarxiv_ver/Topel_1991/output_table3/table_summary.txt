Source: Topel (1991), "Specific Capital, Mobility, and Wages: Wages Rise with Job Seniority,"

==============================================================================
METHODOLOGY
==============================================================================

Two-step estimation procedure:

Step 1 (from Table 2, Model 3):
  First-difference the wage equation to eliminate individual and job-match
  fixed effects. Estimate the combined effect (beta_1 + beta_2) and higher-order
  experience/tenure terms from within-job wage changes.

Step 2 (this table):
  Using the first-step estimates of higher-order terms, construct:
    w*_it = w_it - (beta_1_hat + beta_2_hat) * T_it - g_hat(X_it)
  where the higher-order tenure and experience terms are subtracted from
  the observed wage.

  Then regress w*_it on experience (X_it) using initial experience (X_i0)
  as an instrument to obtain beta_1. The instrument is valid because
  initial experience is determined before any job-match effects.

  beta_2 is recovered as: beta_2 = (beta_1 + beta_2)_step1 - beta_1_step2

Second-step regressors also include:
  - Years of completed schooling
  - Marital status
  - Residence in SMSA
  - Union membership
  - Disability
  - Eight indicators for census region

Cumulative returns calculation:
  Based on main effect beta_2 = .0545 plus higher-order tenure terms from
  Table 2, column 3:
    Tenure^2 (x10^2): -.4592
    Tenure^3 (x10^3): .1846
    Tenure^4 (x10^4): -.0245

  Cumulative return at T years = beta_2 * T + (-.4592/100) * T^2
                                 + (.1846/1000) * T^3 + (-.0245/10000) * T^4

==============================================================================
NOTES
==============================================================================

- The experience effect beta_1 is identified using initial labor market
  experience as an instrument for current experience
- Column (2) comes directly from the first-step estimates (Table 2, col 3)
- Column (3) = Column (2) - Column (1)
- Column (4) measures the OLS bias in estimated within-job wage growth,
  which is the difference between OLS and IV estimates of the linear
  tenure coefficient in a standard cross-sectional wage equation
- The two-step tenure profile is substantially lower than the OLS profile,
  indicating that OLS overstates returns to tenure due to heterogeneity bias
